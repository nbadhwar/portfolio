Plymouth-Canton Community Elementary Schools: Growing Stakeholder Engagement through Digital Platforms

Project Type:

Client Liaison through the University of Michigan School of Information

UX Research | Market Research

My Role:

Conduct interviews with client and stakeholders, observe work practices, and analyze and synthesize qualitative data

Duration:

A semester long project with 3 team members running from September 2020 - December 2020

Background

As the fifth largest school district in Michigan, Plymouth Canton Community Schools (PCCS) provides high quality education to a large community of students. In doing so, the district utilizes a variety of communication methods and mediums in order to deliver information to its community.

PCCS partnered with the University of Michigan for a full analysis of how successfully their digital outlets engage the various stakeholders within the elementary school district.

Problem

Despite the large amount of communication outlets currently employed by the district, there is a lack of two-way engagement between the district and its various stakeholders.

Goal

Discover stakeholder preferences in order to utilize and create communication outlets that increase two-way engagement

Design Process

Background Research


To begin our project, our team worked together to analyze different aspects of our client and problem to better understand the problem. This included four areas of research:

  1. A competitive analysis on the communication outlets utilized by school districts in neighboring cities such as Ann Arbor Public Schools
  2. Background research on Plymouth Canton Community School District and an in depth analysis of the current platforms in use
  3. Research on the importance of communication methods
  4. Research on the importance of parent involvement in childhood education

What we discovered


  1. Other school districts utilized their social media and school websites in a very different way than PCCS. More research was necessary to understand the effectiveness of differences.
  2. PCCS had adapted a model where every piece of information was displayed on every communication outlet, whether it was social media or emails
  3. In order to create a transparent environment with community involvement, adequate communication devices are essential to facilitate these interactions
  4. Children with parental involvement in their academic lives correlates with student-academic success

These various areas of research helped us determine the types of stakeholders we wanted to interview and the areas of focus for our questions.

Interviewing Stakeholders

Based on our background research we identified 3 types of stakeholders we wanted to interview.

Our interview participants and overarching research questions were as follows:
  • 2 Parents: What platforms do parents prefer and what creates preference for a particular platform?
  • 2 Teachers: What are the primary methods of communication used to interact with parents and students, and why are these methods preferred over others?
  • 2 Board Members: How has the community changed or adapted over time in relation to its digital platform use?

Interviews were conducted via Zoom and were around 45-60 minutes in duration. During each interview, participants were encouraged to elaborate on their statements, and express their honest opinions. Two members of our team were in each interview with one leading and asking questions and the other taking detailed notes that were used for later analysis. Qualitative data from each interview was then consolidated into an Affinity Diagram which was used to organize information and uncover common trends identified by stakeholders.

An in depth look at our interview protocol can be found here.

Affinity Diagram

Data from each interview was analyzed using a 4 step process.

  1. Interview notes, recordings, and transcripts were reread and rewatched to derive sets of qualitative data - each set consisting of poignant statements said by the respective participant.
  2. Each set of data per participant was added to a master sheet through Miro - an online visual collaboration tool (essentially a virtual whiteboard). Statements were grouped according to their similarities
  3. The groups were then organized according to a “tree-like” hierarchy and at each level, were given a description outlining the sentiment and general focus of sub-group
  4. The top-tier descriptions were then used to derive our primary findings.

An in-depth look at the Affinity Diagram avilable upon request. The content here is hidden to protect our interviewees.

Findings

After analyzing our interview data we discovered the following findings:

1. Emails are a common and popular form of communication, but the popularity of the platform leads to high volume of emails and some filtering and organizational issues.

  • The popularity of emails leads to an overwhelming amount of emails being sent both privately and publicly
  • Ambiguity around who to email for specific questions and concerns
  • Stakeholders had a difficult time filtering through the mass of emails sent out by the district
  • Email allows for little categorization of messages

2. Since the website is broadly used by the entire district, schools have successfully utilized targeted communication to relay important information.

  • With PCCS being such a large and expansive school district, different groups have been forced to compete for real estate on the primary website. Many schools have found ways to get around this issue by utilizing targeted information campaigns that cater to specific groups and information interests

3. The website has a few organizational issues that make it hard to navigate.

  • There is a large amount of information embedded throughout the site that requires a general idea of where to find specific information
  • Throughout various interviews, users stated the website is “clunky” and “cluttered”

4. Social media is a far-reaching platform that parents turn to for information but can be unprofessional and unreliable.

  • Due to their convictions and familiarity, social media platforms are highly utilized by parents
  • Multiple stakeholders mentioned that the Facebook page is underutilized due to the absence of constructive two-way engagement and lack of professionalism

Recommendations

1. Address over-communication through more targeted communication and narrowing the scope of communication lists.

  • Our suggestion to improve the email list feature would be to narrow the scope of potential lists and transition to increasingly specific lists. Also, limiting the volume of emails sent by each subscription would help to avoid spam-like communications
  • To help resolve the issue of over-communication, we suggest surveying a random sample of parents and inquiring about their preferred method of communication for different types of information

2. Q&A Forum.

  • This forum would best be implemented on a lower level, potentially on a school-by-school basis, and could exist on a platform similar to Reddit
    • Creating a Q&A forum dedicated to answering questions and concerns from stakeholders would highly increase the amount of two-way communication

3. Change the content of social media posts OR increase content regulation.

  • We have found through research and competitive analysis that social media is a powerful tool which generally works well for school districts. Adopting techniques similar to other districts like customizing social media posts and eliminating reusing posts will be helpful in increasing positive engagement and facilitate more positive and constructive two-way communication
  • Having someone act as a moderator on social media to quickly address questions and concerns and take down any negative or unnecessary comments as they come up may help parents feel more comfortable participating in online discussions

4. Consider a reorganization of the website OR develop school-related websites.

  • Through our research we’ve realized that parents and administrators prefer content to be organized according to user-flow.
  • We suggest conducting more user research through surveying and interviews and developing user personas and user experience maps to better understand how, when, and why stakeholders currently engage with the website and how it can be restructured to improve its navigation and organization

Normal Public Library: Website User Experience & Audit

Project Type:

Needs Assessment and Usability Evaluation

Client Liaison through the University of Michigan School of Information

My Role:

Utilizing a variety of usability tests and evaluations in order to determine the success of the library's website in accomplishing user goals

Duration:

A semester long project with 3 team members running from September 2020 - December 2020

Evaluations Performed

Interaction Map

We started off our semester creating an Interaction Map. The goal of this assignment was to generate a static representation of the library's website in order to evaluate all of the actions and possible states. Throughout the process we were able to highlight areas for future tests and evaluations. Additionally, we were able to get a better understanding of the site and noticed many areas of repeated information.




Findings:

Throughout this evaluation, we discovered many areas of information that were repeated throughout the site. We also noted that many pages had blocks of information users were required to read through in order to find information.

An in depth look at our interaction map can be found here.

Interviews

After creating an Interaction Map, our group created an interview protocol. The goal of this assignment was to develop a detailed understanding of our target population and ensure that the NPL website was meeting their needs.

Participants & Instruments

Our target audience for interviews included 4 frequent library users

Interviews were conducted over Zoom and were between 45-60 minutes.

Recruiting Methods

In order to recruit users we created a Hotjar Survey which appeared as a pop-up widget within the NPL library website. If users indicated an interest, they were then sent a screening survey to complete.

Demographics

  • 4 female participants
  • 3 were between the ages of 24-35
  • 1 was between the age of 35-49
  • Each participant had anywhere from 3 years to 15 years of experience as an NPL user

Personas

placeholder

Findings

  1. Frequent users often utilize the physical library space and its attendant features (physical shelves, reference librarians, subject brochures) in order to browse the library collection, and desire an analogous capability within the website.
  2. Frequent users expressed dissatisfaction with their mobile experience.
  3. Frequent users often navigate to Normal Public Library web pages through mediums other than the website.
  4. Frequent users desire clearer organization of the website for different users.

Comparative Evaluation

Examining Normal Public Library's competition allowed us to reveal similarities and differences between products. It also allowed us to note any areas that had a potential for improvement or areas that required further evaluation. Three tiers of relevant comparators were selected.

Tier 1 Comparators:

  • Community based non-profit organizations
  • Have the ability to checkout materials
  • Community programs and events

Tier 2 Comparators:

  • Have materials available for checkout, but lack programs and events
  • Access to materials is limited in some way to the public, either by cost or by membership requirements

Tier 3 Comparators:

  • Play a part in the local community, but are not libraries
  • Offer family-based activities and learning opportunities

Findings

  1. Comparators utilized different layouts for the site's search bar.
  2. Comparators utilized more goal-oriented navigation headings.
  3. Browsing and recommendations for new books is a feature highlighted on many of the various comparator-library websites.
  4. Most comparator libraries utilize the same catalog service, and so there are many similarities between library websites.

Further testing was required on the search bar and goal-oriented headings in order to determine if these difference increased the user-friendliness of the sites.

Surveys

Conducting surveys was an important process in order to gain a broad understanding of the NPL website's current or potential users in order to learn about their needs, attitudes, characteristics, and current practices. We were able to determine the frequency and prevalence of key attitudes, behaviors, and characteristics.


In order to conduct surveys with as many NPL users as possible, our survey was created using Qualtrics and we recruited users through website, using a pop-up advertisement, as well as asking users to participate through the library's newsletters.


According to our client, the mailing list goes out to approximately 27,000 users, with an opening rate of about 20%. This would result in approximately 5,400 potential respondents, from which we gathered 58 responses, resulting in a response rate of just over 1%.

Findings

  1. Infrequent visitors (those who visit once a month or less) to the Normal Public Library website are more likely to be browsing the library’s collection and programming than frequent users
  2. Active users with children under the age of 18 are more likely to use the website to sign up for library events
  3. A sizable minority of responders learn about library events and programs through different NPL mediums such as social media accounts or the newsletter

Heuristic Evaluation

Our team utilized Nielsen’s 10 Heuristics in order to quickly point out any potential usability problems or major flaws throughout the site.

Findings

Overall NPL has a user-friendly website, and our heuristic evaluation only highlighted the following areas with issues:

  • Throughout the site there is very little help or documentation
  • There exists areas for breadcrumbs allowing users to see how they have accessed a specific page, but the links do not work consistently throughout the site
  • Lastly, there is inconsistency in the layout of information on the various pages within the site

Usability Test

Finally, for our last evaluation we conducted usability tests in order to determine how effectively new users were able to navigate the NPL website.

Participants

We recruited 4 individuals who had experience using a library website in the past, but had not used the Normal Public Library website. This blind set up allowed for users who had some idea what they were doing, but provided us with unbiased opinions and clear first impressions on the website.

In order to present our users with tasks, we created various scenarios for the users to follow along with. We watched as they completed each task, taking notes of how each user navigated the site and how successfully they were able to complete each task.

Scenarios

  • You've just moved to Normal Illinois and want to obtain a library card for you and your children. Determine what types of library cards you and your children are eligible for, and the steps you need to take to obtain them.
  • You hope to check out some materials from Normal Public Library and want to determine the availability of curbside pickup. Determine whether curbside pickup is available and the steps necessary to check out a book.
  • You live in Bloomington, a neighboring city to Normal. The book you wish to checkout is not available at your local library. Determine if Normal has the book and whether you are able to check it out.

Findings

  1. Overall, the NPL site is very user-friendly and all of our users were able to complete each task they were provided with.
  2. There is a lack of information on how to perform specific tasks within the NPL website. We also discovered this issue with our heuristic evaluation.
  3. The current search bar set up is confusing to users and we recommend further testing for whether changing the appearance would increase user-engagement.
  4. Finding information within a webpage requires lots of reading and sifting through unrelated information.

Recommendations

Each evaluation was delivered to NPL in the form of a report with information on the methodology, findings, and recommendations. Many of the evaluations reinforced and uncovered similar findings, and our recommendations often overlapped. These recommendations are listed below.

  1. Create and highlight documentation for users to guide them through common website tasks.
  2. Remove features such as "chat with a librarian" that are not currently available to users or highlight hours of operation.
  3. Add links to related webpages or group similar information together in a more concise way to allow users access it easily.
  4. Ensure that all links listed on the website work.
  5. Consider changing the layout of the website to include goal-oriented headings or a more intuitive search bar.
  6. Consider reorganizing information within webpages to make it easier to read and more consistent
  7. Consider replacing the quicklinks on the homepage with more common tasks.
  8. Create a recommendation section on the homepage to aid users who are browsing for materials.

MeatFreed Web Dashboard

Project Type:

Client Liaison through the University of Michigan School of Information

UX Research | Agile Development React.JS | Firebase | Figma | Chart.JS

My Role:

Rotating roles of scrum master, product owner, react developer, ux researcher, and ux designer.

Duration:

A semester long project with 5 team members running from January 2022 - May 2022

Background

MeatFreed is a startup based in the United Kingdom that promotes restaurants and businesses that endorse and sell vegan products. MeatFreed has a mobile app that provides offers and blog posts to their end-users for vegan restaurants, promoting vegan products and local businesses at the same time.

Problem

MeatFreed's business partners lack the visibility to view data on the various posts and offers they provide to end-users through the mobile application that prevents them from connecting and interacting with their customers.

Goal

Our client required a web dashboard that would allow their business partners to view relevant data to understand the impact MeatFreed is having on connecting their business and products to end-users.

Research, Planning, and Design


In order to kick things off, our team began by researching the restaurant industry and various analytic dashboards in order to get a better understanding of what our design should encompass. Our competitive analysis notes can be seen here.


During initial research, our team also adopted an agile development environment where we had weekly sprint ceremonies including product backlog grooming sessions, backlog refinement, daily stand-ups, and retros in order to iteratively create designs and develop code. Our trello board containing our backlog can be seen below.




Our team members rotated through roles of scrum master, product owner, developer, designer, and database engineer in order to complete this project. Every week we assigned a new scrum master and product owner to take on the following weeks tasks where we would groom through our backlog, assign points to stories, pick up various tickets, and make sure the team was on track without any blockers to their tickets. Our design tickets ran a week earlier than their corresponding development tickets to allow for feedback, iterations, and approval from our client. Once approved, the corresponding database and React changes were made to incorporate the wireframes.




We utilized Figma to design mid-fidelity wireframes encompassing the stories in our design tickets, which were then used for reference when developing our React App. An in depth look at our figma file can be seen upon request.

The MeatFreed Web Dashboard


Key Features


Meatfreed Dashboard Users: MeatFreed has a few categories of users that would require various levels of access which in turn would display different information. Based on discussions with our client, we divided the users into the following key roles. Each of these roles was tied to the users account information and only admin users can edit this information.

Admin User:

Anyone who works at MeatFreed, authorized to view engagement data

  • View data associated with all businesses.
  • Add, Update, or Delete Businesses to view the engagement data.
  • Add, Update, or Delete Business Partners as users

Business Partners:

Restaurant and Business Owners associated with MeatFreed

  • View data associated with their own business
  • Add, Update, or Delete common users to allow them to view the engagement data

Common Users:

Anyone else apart from owners: managers or employees associated with a business

  • View data associated with the business they are associated with








Engagement Data: Each type of user has the ability to view user engagement data associated with their restaurant and offers associated with their business. This data is viewable by graphs over a selected period of time, where a user can view the number of clicks their offers have received by end-users.




View Offers: Users can view and sort offers related to their business and filter offers by the number of clicks. Offers are also updated to indicate the expiry date.



Adding and Updating New Users (Admins Only): Admin Users can add, update, or delete in order to add new businesses and business partners in order to add "common users" to manage the account.

Our project concluded after creating this functionality, but we left MeatFreed with a detailed Figma file on recommendations for how to continue to integrate features for future iterations.

CHI 2021 Game Design Competition

Project Type:

  • Product Design
  • UX Research
  • Market Research

Deliverables:

Extended Abstract explaining the game design and research behind the product.

High-fidelity prototype

Duration:

A semester long project with 3 team members running from September 2020 - December 2020

Background

The 2021 ACM CHI Virtual Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems is the premier international conference on Human-Computer Interaction. CHI – pronounced ‘kai’ – is a place where researchers and practitioners gather from across the world to discuss the latest in interactive technology. CHI is generally considered the most prestigious in the field of HCI and attracts thousands of international attendees annually. More information can be found here.

I joined a group of 3 other students at the University of Michigan School of Information to complete in the CHI 2021 Student Game Design Competition. Our team chose the follow category that eventually inspired us to create Strescape: an app that teaches students how to manage stress.

  • Transformative & Transgressive Play: This category attends to the expanding boundaries of play and game design with the emergence of independent games, art games, and political games as well as gamified/playbourised systems that explore the role of play in non-gaming settings. For example, games now play an increasingly important role in areas like education, healthcare, safety, urban planning, sustainability, and other economic, cultural, and societal sectors. Games submitted to this category should draw on these expanding boundaries of game design to create innovative gameplay for non-gaming settings, and critically reflect on challenges.

Abstract

High school is a formative time in which adolescents navigate many changes that can affect their mental health. Many high school students lack the necessary physiological and psychological resources to efficiently cope with stress. As uncontrolled or chronic stress can quickly become a mental and physical health issue, it is important to provide adolescents with the resources to learn how to deal with negative thoughts and emotions. In order to provide a resource that combats this issue, we created Strescape, a mobile application that allows students the resources to escape their reality while developing healthy life-long coping mechanisms. We describe in detail our research and inspiration for the app as well as detailed game play and design aspects.

Our Solution:

We created an app called Strescape with a target audience of high school students. The app teaches students about mindfulness habits while users navigate through a virtual escape room. Each escape room follow a specific theme that encourages relaxation such as a gardening theme.

Personas and Scenarios

We created detailed personas and a scenario to better frame what our project would look like once completed based on our target audience.



Game Prototype and Video

After conducting hours of research on just in time adaptive interventions, mindfullness apps, and current, popular games we created a prototype for Strescape.

AbbVie: Power Platform Developer

Project Type:

Summer and Fall Internship

  • Development
  • UX Design

My Role:

  • Leveraging PowerBI and LeanIX in order to create a life-cycle management dashboard
  • Creating a shopping cart app through PowerApps

Duration:

April 2021 - Present

Background

In April of 2021, I had the opportunity to join AbbVie as a Power Platform Developer where I worked on two projects with their respective teams in order to create designs and mockups, implement the design through PowerBI and PowerApps, present designs to team members and end users, and interview end users on the designs.

Project 1: Power BI Dashboard


During my first project at AbbVie, I worked with the Infrastructure Lifecycle Management Team in order to create a dashboard that would automate the current processes in place for users to update plans regarding the management of servers, databases, etc.

Design Process:
  1. Understanding the current processes in place for tracking and updating end of life items at AbbVie.
  2. Background research on utilizing Power BI and LeanIX in order to better understand how to leverage these tools and create designs.
  3. Interviewing users on the current process in order to uncover any usability issues and discover any wants and needs for future designs in order to incorporate these findings.
  4. Implementing and presenting the dashboard to business users in order to iterate based on design feedback to satisfy their needs.


Interviews:

Informal interviews were taken with any willing participants from various teams at AbbVie at multiple stages throughout the process. The primary user group I focused on included users that were responsible for updating plan information for their respective team.

  1. Users were skeptical on changing the current way they entered plan information.
  2. Users found visuals to be most helpful in order to quickly assess what plans needed to be updated, rather than looking at excel sheets or LeanIX directly.
  3. Users want the ability to filter visuals and excel sheets as efficiently as possible: this includes being able to filter on all relevant fields.
  4. Users want to do as little work as possible when utilizing this dashboard.

Preliminary Designs:

The original process for entering in plans consisted of the iLCM team gathering and updating information manually into excel sheets that were sent to users. Users then had to add their own changes and send the excel sheets back. This was a tedious process that got updated every quarter.

Issues with this design:

  • This is a lengthy and redundant process for the iLCM team
  • The information is only updated every quarter
  • There is only one way for users to update information
